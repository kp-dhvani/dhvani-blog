we multiply ratios, because ratios are, by definition, meant to be multiplied. One octave is 2:1, two octaves are 4:1, three octave are 8:1, four octaves are 16:1. That's cold, hard fact. What happens is that, following just intonation, four stacked major thirds DO NOT EQUAL to an octave, and twelve consecutive perfect fifths DO NOT EQUAL to seven octaves. That's been a well known fact for... a few centuries, I believe? Look up the Pythagorean comma. However, if you follow equal temperament, then three major thirds do equal an octave, and that's because a major third isn't 5:4; it's 2^(3/12):1, which, when multiplied by itself 4 times, does equal 2.

Let's take an example of base frequency of 100Hz and see how does this interval form a scale?

  1. 100 x 3/2 = 150Hz
  2. 150 x 3/2 = 225Hz; this goes outside the octave range so we divide it by 2 to bring it back
    225 / 2 = 112.5Hz
  3. 112.5 x 3/2 = 168.75Hz
  4. 168.75 x 3/2 = 253.125Hz; this goes outside the octave range so we divide it by 2 to bring it back
    253.125 / 2 = 126.5625Hz
